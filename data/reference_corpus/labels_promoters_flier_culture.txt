Record Labels, Promoters, and Flier Culture in Rave History

MAJOR RECORD LABELS BY GENRE

Techno Labels

Metroplex Records — Founded by Juan Atkins in Detroit, 1985. The first techno label. Released Model 500's "No UFOs" and other foundational techno tracks.

Transmat Records — Founded by Derrick May in Detroit, 1986. Released "Strings of Life" (as Rhythim Is Rhythim), one of the most important electronic music tracks ever.

KMS Records — Founded by Kevin Saunderson in Detroit, 1987. Released Inner City's "Big Fun" and "Good Life."

Underground Resistance — Founded by Jeff Mills and "Mad" Mike Banks in Detroit, 1990. Militant, anti-corporate techno collective. Anonymity and collectivism over individual stardom. Released "Transition," "Jupiter Jazz," Galaxy 2 Galaxy.

Axis Records — Founded by Jeff Mills, 1992. Focused, uncompromising techno. Released Waveform Transmission series.

Planet E Communications — Founded by Carl Craig in Detroit, 1991. Eclectic techno blending jazz, ambient, and Detroit soul.

Tresor Records — Founded by Dimitri Hegemann in Berlin, 1991. Bridged Detroit and Berlin techno. Released Jeff Mills, Juan Atkins, Blake Baxter, Drexciya.

Kompakt — Founded by Wolfgang Voigt and Michael Mayer in Cologne, Germany, 1998. Defined the minimal techno and microhouse sound. Pop Ambient compilation series.

Ostgut Ton — Berghain's in-house label, founded 2005. Released Marcel Dettmann, Ben Klock, Steffi, Nd_Baumecker.

Drumcode — Founded by Adam Beyer in Stockholm, 1996. Became the world's best-selling techno label.

Intec Records — Founded by Carl Cox, 1999. Techno label releasing Jon Rundell, Marco Bailey, Harvey McKay, Nicole Moudaber.

M-Plant — Founded by Robert Hood, 1994. Minimal techno. Released Minimal Nation.

House Labels

Trax Records — Founded by Larry Sherman in Chicago, 1984. One of the first house music labels. Released Phuture, Adonis, Marshall Jefferson, Frankie Knuckles.

DJ International — Founded by Rocky Jones in Chicago, 1986. Released Marshall Jefferson's "Move Your Body" and other Chicago house classics.

Strictly Rhythm — Founded in New York, 1989. Released Roger Sanchez, Todd Terry, Armand Van Helden, Erick Morillo.

Defected Records — Founded by Simon Dunmore in London, 1999. Became one of the world's most successful house music labels.

Innervisions — Founded by Dixon, Ame, and Henrik Schwarz in Berlin, 2005. Deep, melodic house and techno.

Cocoon Recordings — Founded by Sven Vath in Frankfurt, 1999. Minimal techno and house.

Drum and Bass Labels

Metalheadz — Founded by Goldie in London, 1994. Dark, sophisticated DnB. Doc Scott, J Majik, Hidden Agenda.

Ram Records — Founded by Andy C in London, 1992. Chase & Status, Sub Focus, Wilkinson.

Good Looking Records — Founded by LTJ Bukem, 1991. Atmospheric/liquid DnB pioneer.

Moving Shadow — Founded 1990. Seminal jungle/DnB label. Omni Trio, Foul Play, Calyx.

Hospital Records — Founded by London Elektricity (Tony Colman) and Chris Goss, 1996. Liquid DnB. Netsky, High Contrast, Logistics.

V Recordings — Founded by Bryan Gee and Jumpin Jack Frost, 1993. Diverse DnB styles.

Full Cycle Records — Founded by Roni Size, DJ Die, Suv, and DJ Krust in Bristol, 1993. Breakbeat-heavy DnB.

Trance Labels

Anjunabeats — Founded by Above & Beyond in London, 2000. Trance and progressive. Sister label Anjunadeep for deeper sounds.

Armada Music — Founded by Armin van Buuren, Maykel Piron, and David Lewis in Amsterdam, 2003. World's largest trance label.

Vandit Records — Founded by Paul van Dyk in Berlin, 1999.

Perfecto Records — Founded by Paul Oakenfold, 1989. Trance and progressive.

PROMOTERS AND EVENT BRANDS

Cream — Founded by James Barton and Andy Carroll in Liverpool, 1992. Superclub brand at Nation nightclub. Launched Creamfields festival.

Ministry of Sound — Founded by James Palumbo in London, 1991. Modeled on Paradise Garage's sound system. Became a global brand with compilation albums, radio, and events.

Gatecrasher — Founded by Scott Bond in Sheffield, 1996. Trance-focused superclub.

Fantazia — Major UK rave brand, early 1990s. Large-scale events at Castle Donington and other venues. Up to 25,000 attendees.

Dreamscape — UK rave brand based at Sanctuary Music Arena, Milton Keynes. One of the longest-running rave brands. Known for hardcore and jungle events.

Raindance — UK rave brand, founded 1989. Hardcore/jungle focused. London area.

Universe — UK rave promoter. Known for "Tribal Gathering" events (1993-1998) which were among the first UK festivals dedicated entirely to electronic music.

Helter Skelter — UK rave brand, early-mid 1990s. Hardcore and happy hardcore events. Famous for elaborate tape packs.

Slammin' Vinyl — UK rave brand specializing in jungle and DnB events.

Progression — UK rave promoter known for trance events.

Life Festival — Irish electronic music festival.

Cocoon — Sven Vath's event brand, held at Amnesia Ibiza and worldwide.

Circoloco — Monday party at DC10, Ibiza. Founded 1999. Underground techno and house.

Music Is Revolution — Carl Cox's residency brand at Space Ibiza (2001-2016).

ENTER. — Richie Hawtin's concept event at Space Ibiza, featuring multiple rooms with different experiences.

RAVE FLIER CULTURE

Rave fliers are among the most distinctive and collectible artifacts of electronic music culture. Before the internet, fliers were the primary method of promoting events. They represent a unique intersection of graphic design, typography, underground culture, and music history.

Design Characteristics

Early rave fliers (1988-1992) featured distinctive visual elements:
- Bright, fluorescent colors (often Day-Glo) designed to be visible under UV/blacklight
- Smiley face motifs (the iconic acid house smiley)
- Psychedelic and fractal imagery
- Bold, hand-drawn typography
- Cut-and-paste collage aesthetic (pre-digital era)
- Often photocopied for mass distribution

Mid-era fliers (1993-1999) evolved with desktop publishing:
- More sophisticated digital design (early Photoshop, Illustrator)
- Brand consistency as rave promoters developed visual identities
- Genre-specific aesthetics: jungle fliers featured darker, more urban designs; trance fliers used cosmic/space imagery; techno fliers were minimal and typographic
- DJ lineup information became more prominent as star DJs emerged
- Venue information often deliberately vague (meeting points, phone lines for directions)

Later fliers (2000-present):
- Professional graphic design matching club/festival branding
- Digital distribution replaced physical fliers for most events
- Physical fliers became collector's items and design artifacts
- Retro flier aesthetics revived for vintage/throwback events

Information Typically Found on Rave Fliers

1. Event name/brand (e.g., "Fantazia," "Dreamscape," "Helter Skelter")
2. Date and day of the week
3. DJ lineup (headliners prominent, supporting acts listed below)
4. MC lineup (especially for jungle/DnB events)
5. Venue name and/or location (sometimes coded or partial for illegal events)
6. Meeting point and info line phone number (for secret location events)
7. Price / ticket information
8. Promoter contact details
9. Sound system credits
10. Record label associations

Notable Flier Artists and Designers

Junior Tomlin — The most celebrated rave flier artist, known for his vibrant, colorful designs for Fantazia, Dreamscape, and many other major UK raves. His style defined the visual language of early 1990s rave culture.

Pez — Prolific rave flier designer whose work appeared on hundreds of events.

Dave Little — Known for hardcore and jungle flier designs.

The visual language of rave fliers influenced graphic design, fashion, and digital art. Collections of original fliers are preserved in archives and museums, including the Museum of Youth Culture in London.

Flier Distribution Methods

Pre-Internet Era:
- Handed out at record shops (essential hubs for rave culture)
- Distributed at previous events
- Mailed to mailing list subscribers
- Placed in clothing shops, cafes, and cultural venues
- Inserted in record sleeves
- Photocopied and passed hand-to-hand

Record shops that served as flier distribution hubs include:
- Black Market Records (London) — House and garage
- Eastern Bloc Records (Manchester) — Techno and house
- Fat Cat Records (London) — Electronica and experimental
- Wax Records (Chicago) — House music
- Record Time (Detroit) — Techno
- Submerge (Detroit) — Techno (also UR distribution)
- Hard Wax (Berlin) — Techno and dub
- Rush Hour (Amsterdam) — House and techno
